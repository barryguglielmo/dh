Part 1: Choose a Language

Choosing a computer coding language can be intimidating. Each language has its own strengths and weaknesses and your personal interests weigh heavily into how you approach this question.

Lucky for you I went through this struggle about a year ago. To help you overcome this first hurdle I decided to put together a list of questions that you should ask yourself (and the all knowing Google machine) before moving too far forward.

Also, keep in mind where you start is not where you end. The vast majority of key concepts will translate from language to language, kind of like how a Canadian can still make sense of a Texan.


Click Below for more:

Your project goals will ultimately determine which direction you will take for your first language. List out what your ideal project would look like and look to see if other people have made that happen and what language they used. Another good idea is to look at jobs you are interested in and seeing what qualifications they require. As a heads up I will focus on Python and R in this site.
I touched upon this idea in the last question, however it is important for your sanity to use a language that is common to the job that you do. Computing is a practical skill, and having problems that you can apply your skill to will help keep you moving forward and keep you excited to learn. So think about how applicable your first language is to your job or schooling.
If you do not want to dig too deep into computer science and are more interested in cranking out statistical models or automating data collection you should use higherlevel programs such as Python and R (obviously my favorites). If you plan to dedicate a lot of time to the computer science side of things think about learning C++ down the line.




Because you are new to all this you should know that there is an important concept of high-level vs low-level programming. An example of extremely high level programming is using Microsoft Excel or making a playlist on Spotify. Low level programming is using C++ or Basic where you need to tell the computer EXACTLY how to behave.




It is for this reason I suggest starting with higher level programming such as Python, or R. Downloads and Tutorials can be found below.

Part 3: Falling With Style

You learn the most through failure, and there is no field in the world that knows this montra as truly as programming. You can only learn by doing and you will fall on your face...like a lot...at the beginning. Do not get discouraged in this time. Look to the Internet for answers. There are large communities out there to help you. Below is a list of some of the forums that I have turned to time and time again.

TutorialsPoint is a great place to get an understanding of the core concepts as the apply to Both R and Python. I strongly encourage you to check them out!
Stack Overflow is a comunity of coders asking questions and getting answers. Chances are someone has run into the exact same problem. You can use your favorite search engine or search their site at StackOverflow.com
Yes, it is that simple. YouTube will help you so much figuring things out, just Try not to get TOO distracted by all the amazing videos of sloths.
