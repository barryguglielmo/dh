LEUCINE RICH REPEAT LGI FAMILY MEMBER 2
(LGI2)



Intro

The LGI2 gene codes for the LGI2 protein which has two isoforms (let us call them LGI2(1x) and LGI2(2x)). For this project assume that I am reffering to LGI2(1x) unless specifically stated.

The LGI2 protein has been observed to play a role in the development of epilepsy. LGI2 is part of the LGI family which has 4 known members (LGI1, LGI2, LGI3, and LGI4) and the LGI family is part of the Leucine-rich repeat (LRR) superfamily. The LRR superfamily has characteristic α/β horseshoe fold motif [1].

The LGI family plays a variety of roles in neurological function and development. The LGI2 gene is most closely related to its sister gene LGI1 which is known to play a role in epilepsy, which is what first lead researchers to pursue LGI2 as a candidate protein for epilepsy studies.

For my project I decided to utilize the web based tools to do my own analysis of LGI’s DNA, mRNA, Protein structure, protein-protein interactions. To anylize LGI2’s DNA and mRNA I used MUSCLE, Emboss, and Python for pairwise and multiple sequence alignments. To better understand LGI2’s protein structure I used MotifFinder and SignalP. To better understand the current research and protein-protein interactions I used NCBI Gene, PubMed, and Protein databases.

Lastly, LGI2 is conserved in several different organisms, but most of the LGI2 studies outside of Homo sapiens have been done with dogs. I wanted to know if dogs were in fact the most similar non-primate organisms for LGI2 study. The results of my analysis are below. Motifs and Domains


Figure 1. This image was taken from "Structural principles of leucine-rich repeat (LRR) proteins"; Purevjav Enkhbayar et al.



Figure 1 demonstrates the LRR α/β horseshoe fold motif. These folds are typically 20–30 amino acids long and these domains fold into a horseshoe shape with a parallel β-sheet on the concave face and with various secondary structures, including α-helix, 310-helix, and pII helix on the convex face [1].

To find where these domains (or more accurately motifs) exist in the LGI2 protein I used MotifFinder. From Figure 2 we can see that there were 5 motifs found. There were 4 LRR motifs found (LRR_4, LRR_5, LRR_8 and LRR_9) and 1 EPTP motif. Turning again to literature The EPTP domain has been implicated with LGI1/Epitempin gene that causes autosomal dominant lateral temporal epilepsy [2]. This suggests that mutations or alterations in the EPTP domain of LGI2 may have a similar effect.

In order to better understand the differences between the two isoforms I ran MotifFinder on both of them. There were no missing motifs even though LGI2(2x) is 44 amino acids shorter than LGI2(1x).





Though all motifs are present in both LGI2(1x) and LGI2(2x) this does not mean that the sequences for each motif are identical. To check if they were identical I used Python to align the regions in which we find each motif. This was done by using Biopython and SeqIO to parse the fasta files obtained on RefSeq then defining a simple function that would do a local alignment of the sequences. My python code can be found in the "Python Code" link of the menu.

There were no observable differences in motif regions. This would suggest that the isoforms that were provided through RefSeq are the common non-disease related forms of the LGI2 mRNA and Protein. As a final precaution I decided to use BLAST Nucleotide to do a Smith and Waterman local alignment of the two isoforms of LGI2. Figure 3 shows the results of the BLAST alignment and the fact that there are no differences between the two isoforms in the motif regions.

Mutations

Because both isoforms of the LGI2 protein in the NCBI database appear to have the same necessary motifs and domains I need to turn back to literature to figure out what mutations might be disease causing. A study by Vachiranee Limviphuvadh et al Found that there were 4 SNP mutations in the LGI2 gene that were associated with epilepsy. These mutations are K347E, V420E, R444Q, and Q452R. All of these SNP's are located in epilepsy-associated repeat regions (EAR) propeller region and presumably interact with A Disintegrin and metalloproteinase domain (ADAM) metalloproteases. Additionally SNP 124T in the N-terminal region of the signal peptide was found to be associated with epilepsy [3].



Figure 4



To see if I could find regions of the protein that were most likely to be spliced, and hopefully find some overlap with the known SNPs I utilized the SignalP web software.

I found a splice site between amino acid positions 22 and 23 when looking at the first 70 amino acids, however when looking at the protein in its entirety I found that there was also a splice site between positions 29 and 30. I was hoping to see a splice site in the EPTP domain, however this was not the case. Lining the results of MotifFinder with SignalP we can see that these splice sites are in the N-terminal region, with a slight spike in the S and Y scores where the LRR domains appear to overlap.



Figure 5



LGI2 is secreted in many different cell types, however we are most interested in its function in brain due to its relationship with epilepsy. In brain LGI2 is secreted by neurons into the extracellular space and cell lysate and acts on metalloproteinase-lacking members of the ADAM family of neuronal receptors, which function in synapse remodeling. It is also important to note that LGI2 truncation prevents secretion and ADAM interaction.

The beta propeller of LGI2 can be seen in Figure 6 a. Though the LGI2 gene is undergoing much study I have not been able to find any literature that has pieced the puzzle of LGI2's role in epilepsy in its entirety. From further reading I found that the beta propeller domain is conserved in LGI family, and that this is the part of the protein that most likely interacts with the ADAM protein and thus mutations in the beta propeller can be detrimental to the proteins function [4].

Model Organisms

Interestingly, LGI linked epilepsy has also been observed in dogs, which got me thinking: How good of a model organism is a dog for LGI2 study? To better understand the relationship of the LGI2 gene across species and determine if it was well conserved I made a few multiple sequence alignments of mRNA. mRNA is a better proxy than DNA for the actual gene products because mRNA does not contain introns, so the full mRNA sequence is translated into the amino acid sequence of the protein. MUSCLE was used to make multiple sequence alignments.

For the first alignment I gave the software an easy test by comparing the mRNA from animal pairs that are known to be similar; Human, Chimpanzee, House Mouse, and Norwegain Rat. The mRNA sequences were obtained from the NCBI RefSeq database. Figure 7 shows the clatogram the MUSCLE software built.



Figure 7

From figure 7 we can see that the human and chimp LGI2 mRNA are more similar to each other than they are to the mouse and rat. However, that is no surprise! Epilepsy in dogs has been found to be associated with LGI2 mutations, so I wanted to explore the possibility of using different animal models of LGI2 mutations. To test this I used the mRNA code for Human, Rat, Chimp, Dog, and Cow.


Figure 8

From figure 8 it appears that humans LGI2 is more similar to a rat than to the other organisms. This makes sense seeing as we often use rats for genetic studies. It is comforting to see that the rat LGI2 mRNA is not drastically different than ours. To see if there were any major discrepancies between humans and rats I did a pair wise alignment using Emboss Protein Needleman and Wunch.

A pairwise alignment was done between Human and Rat then for Human and Dog. From the mRNA alignments it appeared that Rat would have a more similar prtoein, however, the pairwise alignments suggest that the LGI2 protein of the dogs are more closely related to human. I believe this is why there have been studies connecting dogs a pairwise alignment was done for the organisms LGI2 protein. After doing these pairwise alignments dogs seem to be more similar to humans than rats. See figure 9.



Figure 9

In conclusion rat's have a more similar LGI2 mRNA sequence to a human's mRNA than dog's mRNA, however, the LGI2 protein of dog's is more similar to human's than a rat's protein. I would imagine this was taken into consideration when studies on LGI linked epilepsy began.


Discussion

LGI1 and LGI2 have been found to be implicated in epilepsy both in humans and in dogs. Mutations in the EPTP or beta propeller domain can result in phenotypic alterations of the host organism. In order to find definitive genetic markers of LGI2 associated epilepsy an epidemiological study of LGI2 mRNA would be necessary. In order for me to further my own study of this gene I would need access to both genetic and phenotype records. The European database on epilepsy called Epilepsiae is one such database that could be useful and contains 100 annotated datasets thus far.
